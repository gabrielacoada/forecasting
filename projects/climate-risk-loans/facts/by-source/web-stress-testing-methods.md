## Fact 1
- **Claim**: Acharya et al. (2023) propose a climate stress testing framework that uses a two-stage approach: (1) mapping climate scenarios to macroeconomic variables (GDP growth, unemployment, inflation, interest rates) using integrated assessment models, and (2) using these macro variables as inputs to standard credit risk models to estimate bank losses on loan portfolios.
- **Source**: https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q5, Q6

## Fact 2
- **Claim**: Acharya et al. (2023) emphasize that the key methodological challenge in climate stress testing is the "double translation" problem: first translating climate/policy paths into macroeconomic outcomes, and then translating macroeconomic outcomes into bank-level financial impacts (loan losses, capital depletion). Each translation step introduces model uncertainty.
- **Source**: https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q5, Q6

## Fact 3
- **Claim**: Acharya et al. (2023) find that under disorderly transition scenarios (e.g., delayed policy action followed by abrupt carbon pricing), bank loan losses can be significantly higher than under orderly transition scenarios, because the sudden economic adjustment creates sharper GDP contractions and unemployment spikes that amplify credit risk.
- **Source**: https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q5, Q7

## Fact 4
- **Claim**: Acharya et al. (2023) argue that existing bank stress testing frameworks (like CCAR/DFAST) can be adapted for climate stress testing by replacing the macroeconomic scenario paths with climate-scenario-conditioned macro paths, while retaining the same credit loss modeling infrastructure. This is referred to as a "bridge" or "satellite model" approach.
- **Source**: https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q5, Q6

## Fact 5
- **Claim**: Jung et al. (2024) measure U.S. banks' exposures to climate transition risks by analyzing the composition of bank loan portfolios across industries with differing carbon intensities. They find that banks with higher concentrations of lending to carbon-intensive sectors (energy, utilities, transportation, manufacturing) face larger potential losses under transition scenarios that impose carbon pricing.
- **Source**: https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1058.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q5, Q7

## Fact 6
- **Claim**: Jung et al. (2024) use NGFS climate scenario outputs -- particularly carbon price paths and GDP trajectories -- to estimate how transition risk propagates to bank loan portfolios. They find that transition risk affects commercial and industrial (C&I) loans more directly than consumer loans, because C&I borrowers are more exposed to sector-specific carbon pricing and energy cost shocks.
- **Source**: https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1058.pdf

- **Relevant to**: Q5, Q7

## Fact 7
- **Claim**: The NGFS (Network for Greening the Financial System) provides six core climate scenarios organized into four quadrants based on two dimensions: (1) whether the transition is orderly vs. disorderly, and (2) whether climate goals are met vs. not met. The six scenarios are: Net Zero 2050, Below 2C, Divergent Net Zero (orderly/disorderly meeting goals), Delayed Transition, NDCs (Nationally Determined Contributions), and Current Policies.
- **Source**: https://www.ngfs.net/en/ngfs-climate-scenarios
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q5, Q7

## Fact 8
- **Claim**: NGFS scenarios are produced by three integrated assessment model (IAM) families: GCAM (Global Change Analysis Model), REMIND-MAgPIE, and MESSAGEix-GLOBIOM. Each model family produces different macro-financial variable paths for the same scenario narrative, reflecting structural uncertainty in how climate-economy interactions are modeled.
- **Source**: https://www.ngfs.net/en/ngfs-climate-scenarios
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q5, Q7

## Fact 9

Under "Net Zero 2050," carbon prices rise steeply (reaching $200-800/tCO2 by 2050 depending on the IAM), creating significant transition risk but limiting warming to 1.5C.
- **Source**: https://www.ngfs.net/en/ngfs-climate-scenarios

This creates the sharpest short-term GDP contraction of all NGFS scenarios, as the economy must adjust more rapidly to higher carbon costs, resulting in higher unemployment and larger credit losses in the near term.

## Fact 19
- **Claim**: The ECB's climate stress test methodology used a "top-down" component (ECB models applied uniformly) and a "bottom-up" component (banks' own internal models). Banks were required to project credit losses by mapping NGFS scenario macro variables to probability of default (PD) and loss given default (LGD) for their loan portfolios. Many banks reported significant data gaps and methodological limitations.
- **Source**: https://www.bankingsupervision.europa.eu/ecb/pub/pdf/ssm.climate_stress_test_report.20220708~2e3cc0571f.en.pdf

## Fact 20
- **Claim**: In the ECB climate stress test, banks that had not yet developed dedicated climate risk models relied on "bridge equations" or "satellite models" that link NGFS macro variables (GDP growth, carbon prices, unemployment) to credit risk parameters (PD, LGD). This approach extends conventional macro-financial stress testing by adding climate-specific variables to existing regression-based frameworks.
- **Source**: https://www.bankingsupervision.europa.eu/ecb/pub/pdf/ssm.climate_stress_test_report.20220708~2e3cc0571f.en.pdf

## Fact 21
- **Claim**: A common forecasting approach for climate stress testing uses a VAR (Vector Autoregression) or structural VAR framework where macroeconomic variables (GDP growth, unemployment, interest rates, inflation) are modeled jointly, and then climate scenario paths are imposed as conditional forecasts or exogenous shocks. The VAR framework is favored because it captures dynamic interdependencies among macro variables while remaining transparent and interpretable.
- **Source**: General methodology described in BIS Working Papers and NGFS technical documentation; see also Acharya et al. (2023)
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q5

## Fact 22
- **Claim**: The "bridge equation" or "satellite model" approach to climate stress testing involves two steps: (1) estimating reduced-form equations that link loan portfolio outcomes (e.g., loan growth, charge-off rates) to macroeconomic drivers (GDP, unemployment, interest rates), and (2) feeding climate-scenario-conditioned macro paths into these equations to generate scenario-conditional loan forecasts. This approach is widely used because it separates the climate-to-macro translation from the macro-to-loans translation.
- **Source**: NGFS technical documentation; ECB methodology papers; Acharya et al. (2023)
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q5

## Fact 23
- **Claim**: For this specific project, the recommended forecasting approach involves: (1) using historical FRED data to estimate a VAR or set of regression equations linking BUSLOANS/CONSUMER to macroeconomic variables (GDP, unemployment, CPI, interest rates), (2) mapping NGFS scenario variables (carbon prices, GDP paths, energy costs) to the macro variables used in the model, and (3) generating conditional loan forecasts under each NGFS scenario. This two-stage approach aligns with both the academic literature and regulatory practice.
- **Source**: Synthesis of Acharya et al. (2023), Jung et al. (2024), and project requirements in CLAUDE.md
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q5

## Fact 28
- **Claim**: A key methodological challenge identified across all climate stress testing exercises is the frequency mismatch between climate scenario data (typically annual, long-horizon) and financial data (quarterly or monthly, shorter history). Solutions include: temporal aggregation of financial data to annual frequency, interpolation of scenario data to higher frequencies, or using bridge equations that operate at mixed frequencies (MIDAS regression).
- **Source**: NGFS technical documentation; ECB methodology papers; project CLAUDE.md
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q5

## Fact 32
- **Claim**: Consumer loan portfolios are primarily affected by climate risk through indirect macroeconomic channels: climate-driven GDP contractions increase unemployment, which reduces household income and increases consumer loan defaults. Physical risks can also directly impair consumer collateral (e.g., property damage reducing home equity), while transition risks affect consumers through energy cost increases that reduce disposable income.
- **Source**: Synthesis of ECB climate stress test findings and Acharya et al. (2023)
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q5, Q7

## Fact 33
- **Claim**: Commercial and industrial (C&I) loan portfolios are affected by climate risk through both direct and indirect channels. Direct channels include: increased operating costs from carbon pricing, stranded assets in fossil fuel sectors, and physical damage to commercial property. Indirect channels include: GDP contractions reducing business revenues, supply chain disruptions from extreme weather, and tightening of financial conditions as markets reprice climate risk.
- **Source**: Jung et al. (2024); Acharya et al. (2023)
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q5, Q7

## Fact 34
- **Claim**: The IMF's Global Financial Stability Report (2022) found that a disorderly transition scenario could increase corporate loan default rates by 3-4 percentage points for firms in the most carbon-intensive sectors, compared to a 0.5-1.0 percentage point increase for firms in low-carbon sectors. At the aggregate banking system level, the IMF estimated that disorderly transition could reduce bank capital ratios by 1-4 percentage points depending on portfolio composition.
- **Source**: IMF Global Financial Stability Report, October 2022, Chapter 5

- **Relevant to**: Q6, Q7

## Fact 35
- **Claim**: Transparent, explainable models for climate stress testing typically include: (1) linear regression / OLS-based bridge equations linking macro variables to loan outcomes, (2) VAR models capturing multivariate macro dynamics, (3) error correction models (VECM) when variables share long-run cointegrating relationships, and (4) panel regression models when cross-sectional data (e.g., across sectors or regions) is available. Black-box ML models are generally discouraged by regulators due to lack of interpretability.
- **Source**: Synthesis of ECB, BoE, and Fed methodology guidance; Acharya et al. (2023)
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q5

## Fact 36
- **Claim**: The NGFS scenarios use the NiGEM (National Institute Global Econometric Model) macroeconomic model to translate IAM outputs into standard macro-financial variables. NiGEM is a global structural econometric model that produces country-level GDP, inflation, unemployment, interest rates, exchange rates, and asset prices conditioned on the climate/energy paths from the IAMs. This is the "macro-financial bridge" in the NGFS framework.
- **Source**: NGFS technical documentation; https://www.ngfs.net/en/ngfs-climate-scenarios
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q5, Q6

## Fact 37
- **Claim**: For a simplified, market-based climate stress testing prototype using publicly available data (as described in the project document), the recommended approach is a conditional forecasting framework: (1) estimate a reduced-form model of loan dynamics using historical macro data, (2) take NGFS-provided macro variable paths as given (exogenous scenario inputs), and (3) compute scenario-conditional loan forecasts by feeding the NGFS paths through the estimated model. This avoids the need to re-estimate the climate-to-macro translation, leveraging the NGFS's own macro modeling.
- **Source**: Synthesis from project document methodology and Acharya et al. (2023) framework

## Fact 40
- **Claim**: Under the NGFS Net Zero 2050 scenario, the orderly transition involves gradual carbon price increases starting immediately, reaching high levels by mid-century. GDP growth is modestly lower than current policies in the near term but avoids severe physical risk damages long-term. Under Delayed Transition, near-term GDP growth is closer to baseline but faces a sharp contraction when abrupt policies are imposed after 2030, creating a "disorderly adjustment" with peak GDP losses around 2035-2040.

## Fact 41
- **Claim**: The Bank for International Settlements (BIS) has published multiple working papers emphasizing that standard macroeconomic models may underestimate the tail risks associated with climate change, because climate impacts involve nonlinearities, tipping points, and irreversibilities that are poorly captured by linear VAR or DSGE models. This suggests that while VAR-based stress testing is a reasonable starting point, results should be interpreted as lower bounds on potential climate-related losses.
- **Source**: BIS Working Papers on climate-related financial risks (multiple, 2020-2024)
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q5

## Fact 42
- **Claim**: A practical challenge for using NGFS scenarios in loan portfolio forecasting is that NGFS macro variables are provided as levels or growth rates at annual frequency, while FRED loan data (BUSLOANS, CONSUMER) is available at monthly or quarterly frequency. Common solutions include: (a) aggregating FRED data to annual frequency to match NGFS, (b) using cubic spline or linear interpolation to disaggregate NGFS data to quarterly/monthly, or (c) using mixed-frequency models like MIDAS (Mixed Data Sampling) regression.
- **Source**: NGFS technical documentation; standard econometric methodology
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q5

## Fact 43

Banks with more diversified loan portfolios showed lower concentration risk to climate transition shocks.
- **Source**: https://www.federalreserve.gov/publications/climate-scenario-analysis-exercise.htm

## Fact 44
- **Claim**: The NGFS "NDCs" (Nationally Determined Contributions) scenario models a world where countries implement only their current pledges under the Paris Agreement, leading to approximately 2.5C of warming by 2100. This scenario falls between the orderly transition scenarios and Current Policies in terms of both transition risk and physical risk, making it a useful "middle ground" benchmark for stress testing.
- **Source**: NGFS scenario documentation
- **Confidence**: high

## Fact 45
- **Claim**: Across all major climate stress testing exercises (ECB, BoE, Fed pilot), a consistent finding is that the short-term costs of transition (measured in loan losses, GDP impact, and capital depletion) are lower under orderly/early action scenarios than under disorderly/delayed action scenarios, even though both achieve similar long-term climate outcomes. This supports the "act early" policy message -- delay increases financial stability risks.
- **Source**: Synthesis of ECB (2022), BoE CBES (2022), and Fed CSA pilot (2024) results
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q6, Q7

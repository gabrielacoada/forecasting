## Fact 1
- **Claim**: The authors build on sectoral estimates from three general equilibrium models -- Jorgenson et al. (2018) using the IGEM model, Goulder and Hafstead (2018) using the E3 model, and the G-Cubed model estimates of the NGFS (2022a) scenarios -- to measure banks' exposures to climate transition risks. This is distinct from the common approach of using carbon emissions as a proxy for transition risk.
- **Source**: Jung et al. (2024) "U.S. Banks' Exposures to Climate Transition Risks", SR 1058, pages 1-2
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q2, Q5, Q6

## Fact 2
- **Claim**: The paper uses loan-level data from the Federal Reserve's Y-14 data collection. Since 2011, large banks with more than $50 billion in assets are required to report detailed information on most types of C&I loans on their balance sheet with a commitment of $1 million or more. The data covers publicly listed and large private borrowers as well as medium-sized businesses.
- **Source**: Jung et al. (2024) "U.S. Banks' Exposures to Climate Transition Risks", SR 1058, page 2
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q4, Q6

## Fact 3
- **Claim**: The average bank's exposure to transition risks as of 2023 does not exceed 14% of their loan portfolios under all of the scenarios considered, including the strictest policies and most adverse scenarios. For reference, banks projected a 7% C&I loss rate under the 2023 Stress Test severely adverse scenario.
- **Source**: Jung et al. (2024) "U.S. Banks' Exposures to Climate Transition Risks", SR 1058, page 3
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q7, Q8

## Fact 4
- **Claim**: According to Jorgenson et al. (2018), the average bank's exposure to transition risks varies between 0.5% and 3.5% as of 2023, with the latter emerging under the policy that sets an initial carbon tax of $50 which grows at 5% per year.
- **Source**: Jung et al. (2024) "U.S. Banks' Exposures to Climate Transition Risks", SR 1058, pages 3-4

## Fact 5

## Fact 6

## Fact 7
- **Claim**: Commonly used carbon emissions can explain at most 60% of bank exposures to transition risks estimated from general equilibrium models. At least 40% of the variation in banks' exposures to transition risk is not explained by banks' emission funding. This highlights the value of using forward-looking measures of transition risks derived from general equilibrium models.
- **Source**: Jung et al. (2024) "U.S. Banks' Exposures to Climate Transition Risks", SR 1058, pages 4, 29
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q2, Q4, Q5

## Fact 12
- **Claim**: The three general equilibrium models used differ in important ways. Jorgenson et al. (2018) uses the IGEM-N model which structures the economy around 36 industries based on NAICS codes. Goulder and Hafstead (2018) uses the E3 model covering 35 industries. NGFS (2022a) uses the G-Cubed model covering 12 industries (with an extended version covering 20).
- **Source**: Jung et al. (2024) "U.S. Banks' Exposures to Climate Transition Risks", SR 1058, pages 9, 13, 15, 16
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q1, Q5

## Fact 13
- **Claim**: Trucost data on carbon emissions is used, available from 2013:Q1 until 2021:Q4. Trucost provides information on greenhouse gas emissions in millions of tons, collected from annual reports and firm disclosures in the Carbon Disclosure Project (CDP). The authors focus on scope 1 emissions (direct emissions from establishments controlled by the company).
- **Source**: Jung et al. (2024) "U.S. Banks' Exposures to Climate Transition Risks", SR 1058, page 9
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q4

## Fact 14
- **Claim**: Industry-level scope 1 emissions are computed for the average firm in the industry (weighted by the firm's total assets from Compustat). Bank carbon emissions funding is computed as the average of each borrower's industry-level emissions, weighted by the amount of lending to that borrower. Bank emission intensity is defined as bank emissions funding scaled by bank total assets.
- **Source**: Jung et al. (2024) "U.S. Banks' Exposures to Climate Transition Risks", SR 1058, pages 9-10
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q4, Q5

## Fact 15
- **Claim**: The core exposure measure is: Exposure_{b,t}^P = sum_j w_{b,j,t} * Markdown_j^P, where w_{b,j,t} is the share of bank b's loans made to industry j at time t, and Markdown_j^P is the expected percentage drop in output or profits for industry j under policy P. The measure assumes loan values are impaired proportionally to the drop in expected output or profits of the borrower's industry.
- **Source**: Jung et al. (2024) "U.S. Banks' Exposures to Climate Transition Risks", SR 1058, page 10
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q2, Q5

## Fact 16
- **Claim**: The authors assume a static portfolio of credit exposures -- that banks maintain the same industry shares of their loan portfolios over time. This implicitly assumes that when a loan matures, the bank will either refinance or extend a loan to another borrower in that industry.
- **Source**: Jung et al. (2024) "U.S. Banks' Exposures to Climate Transition Risks", SR 1058, pages 2, 11
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q5, Q4

## Fact 17
- **Claim**: The industry-level estimates from general equilibrium models are matched with bank loans in the Y14 data based on NAICS crosswalks provided in Tables A.1, A.2, and A.3. For IGEM-N, loans that do not map to an IGEM industry are dropped, amounting to 1.2% of loans in 2023:Q1. For E3, 6.9% of loans are excluded. For NGFS 12-industry mapping, 3.6% are excluded.
- **Source**: Jung et al. (2024) "U.S. Banks' Exposures to Climate Transition Risks", SR 1058, pages 10, 13, 15, 16
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q4, Q5

The second set of estimates varies the form of income recycling (lump sum, capital tax cut, labor tax cut) while keeping the initial tax at $25 and growth rate at 5%.

## Fact 21

The model provides estimates of changes in US industry profits for 35 industries over an infinite time horizon.

## Fact 22
- **Claim**: Under the Goulder and Hafstead (2018) model, the average bank's loan value is expected to fall by around 1% under the first three scenarios (lump sum redistribution, payroll tax cut, individual income tax cut). When a corporate tax cut is introduced, the exposure is negative (-1%), meaning the average bank would benefit from the policy because profits actually increase for 20 out of 35 industries.

## Fact 23
- **Claim**: The NGFS (2022a) scenarios use the G-Cubed model which endogenously estimates what carbon tax is needed to achieve specific climate goals. Three scenarios are modeled: (1) Current policy -- $3.72 carbon tax in 2021 growing to $26.50 in 2050; (2) Orderly transition -- $16.75 tax in 2021 growing to $119.14 in 2050, proceeds used for infrastructure and government debt; (3) Disorderly transition -- no tax until 2030, then $31.52 growing to $121.97 in 2050, proceeds as lump sum dividend to households.
- **Source**: Jung et al. (2024) "U.S. Banks' Exposures to Climate Transition Risks", SR 1058, page 16
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)

## Fact 24
- **Claim**: Banks' exposures based on NGFS scenarios are much higher than from the other two models. Under the orderly transition, the average bank's loan value decreases by about 6% as of 2023; under the disorderly transition, it decreases by about 6.5%. The exposure under current policy is about 2%. Exposures based on the orderly and disorderly scenarios have fallen by about 3 percentage points since 2014, primarily driven by increased lending to the "services" sector.

## Fact 30
- **Claim**: The authors present three alternative approaches for translating industry-level output/profit changes into bank exposures: (1) one-to-one proportional relationship between industry effects and bank exposures; (2) adjusting for historical probability of default (PD) and loss given default (LGD) from Y-14 data; (3) assuming the riskiest industries (top decile or top two deciles) lose their entire value.
- **Source**: Jung et al. (2024) "U.S. Banks' Exposures to Climate Transition Risks", SR 1058, page 3
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q2, Q5

## Fact 31
- **Claim**: Adjusting for bank loan payoff structures (PD and LGD) reduces bank exposures by between 1% and 5%. In all models, bank loan portfolios are expected to drop by about 1-2% after adjusting for loan payoff structures as of 2023. The adjustment is largest (about 5%) for the NGFS disorderly scenario.

## Fact 32
- **Claim**: The loss-adjusted exposure measure calculates Loss_{l,t} as the product of loss given default and probability of default found in the Y14 data. For each industry, a regression estimates how a percentage change in output or profits affects expected loan loss: Loss_{l,t} = alpha_j + beta_j * log(Sales_{l,t}) + epsilon_{l,t}.

## Fact 49
- **Claim**: The paper's approach differs from existing studies in a key way: existing studies capture banks' exposures by considering the level of CO2 emissions of borrowers (industries) they have granted loans to. By contrast, this paper capitalizes on general equilibrium industry-level estimates which are forward-looking and capture all facets of the transition, not just emissions. "Emissions are backward-looking and are only one of the dimensions that will be affected in a transition to a low-carbon economy."
- **Source**: Jung et al. (2024) "U.S. Banks' Exposures to Climate Transition Risks", SR 1058, page 6
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q2, Q5

## Fact 50
- **Claim**: The ECB climate stress test (European Central Bank, 2023) found that expected losses of European banks' credit portfolios are minimal, around 0.7% of the total loan exposure under both the accelerated and delayed transition scenarios, and 0.9% under the late push scenario. In the authors' baseline scenario, average bank losses range from 1.8% to 6.4%; when factoring in PDs and LGDs to align with the ECB's methodology, estimated losses range between 0.5% and 1.3%.
- **Source**: Jung et al. (2024) "U.S. Banks' Exposures to Climate Transition Risks", SR 1058, pages 3, 5-6
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q6, Q7

## Fact 1
- **Claim**: The Network for Greening the Financial System (NGFS) is a coalition of central banks and financial supervisors (over 130 members as of 2023) that develops climate scenarios to help the financial sector assess climate-related risks. The NGFS scenarios are not forecasts or predictions; they are "what-if" explorations of how climate change and climate policy might evolve and affect the economy and financial system.
- **Source**: https://www.ngfs.net/en/about-us/governance/origin-and-purpose
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q1

## Fact 2
- **Claim**: The NGFS scenario framework organizes scenarios along two key dimensions: (1) the level of physical risk (driven by temperature outcomes) and (2) the level of transition risk (driven by the speed, timing, and coordination of climate policy). This creates a 2x2-style matrix, though the NGFS defines six representative scenarios rather than four.
- **Source**: https://www.ngfs.net/sites/default/files/medias/documents/ngfs_climate_scenarios_for_central_banks_and_supervisors_phase_iv.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q1

## Fact 3
- **Claim**: The six NGFS representative scenarios (as of Phase IV, November 2023) are: (1) **Net Zero 2050** -- an ambitious scenario that limits global warming to 1.5C through immediate and stringent climate policies, reaching net-zero CO2 emissions around 2050; (2) **Below 2C** -- a scenario with gradually increasing stringency of climate policy, limiting warming to below 2C by 2100; (3) **Divergent Net Zero** -- reaches net zero by 2050 but with higher costs due to divergent policies across sectors (some sectors decarbonize faster while others lag), resulting in greater transition risk; (4) **Delayed Transition** -- assumes no new climate policy until 2030, after which strong policies are introduced abruptly to still limit warming to below 2C, creating a sharp disorderly transition; (5) **Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs)** -- assumes countries fulfill their current pledges under the Paris Agreement but do not increase ambition, leading to roughly 2.5C warming; (6) **Current Policies** -- assumes no new climate policies beyond those already implemented, leading to roughly 3C+ warming and severe physical risks.
- **Source**: https://www.ngfs.net/sites/default/files/medias/documents/ngfs_climate_scenarios_for_central_banks_and_supervisors_phase_iv.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q1

## Fact 4
- **Claim**: The six NGFS scenarios fall into three broad categories based on their risk profile: (a) **Orderly scenarios** (Net Zero 2050, Below 2C) -- early, gradual policy action minimizes both physical and transition risks; (b) **Disorderly scenarios** (Divergent Net Zero, Delayed Transition) -- late, sudden, or uncoordinated policy action leads to high transition risk; (c) **Hot house world scenarios** (NDCs, Current Policies) -- insufficient policy action leads to severe physical risks from high warming, though transition risk is low because little policy change occurs.
- **Source**: https://www.ngfs.net/sites/default/files/medias/documents/ngfs_climate_scenarios_for_central_banks_and_supervisors_phase_iv.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q1

## Fact 5
- **Claim**: The NGFS scenarios are produced by three Integrated Assessment Model (IAM) families: (1) **GCAM** (Global Change Analysis Model, developed by PNNL/JGCRI), (2) **REMIND-MAgPIE** (developed by PIK, Potsdam Institute for Climate Impact Research), and (3) **MESSAGEix-GLOBIOM** (developed by IIASA). Each model family produces its own set of outputs for each scenario, and they may differ in their projections because of different modeling assumptions, sectoral detail, and regional disaggregation.
- **Source**: https://data.ene.iiasa.ac.at/ngfs/#/downloads
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q1

## Fact 6
- **Claim**: The three IAM families differ in their structural approach: GCAM is a partial-equilibrium model with detailed energy-technology and land-use modules; REMIND-MAgPIE is a general-equilibrium model that optimizes the energy-economy system intertemporally; MESSAGEix-GLOBIOM is a systems-engineering optimization model with detailed technology representation. These structural differences mean that the same scenario narrative (e.g., Net Zero 2050) can produce different numerical paths for variables like GDP, carbon prices, and energy mix depending on the model family used.
- **Source**: https://data.ene.iiasa.ac.at/ngfs/#/downloads (model documentation)
- **Confidence**: medium (structural summaries simplified from detailed model documentation)
- **Relevant to**: Q1

## Fact 7
- **Claim**: The NGFS scenario data portal (hosted by IIASA) provides the following major categories of variables: (a) **Emissions** -- CO2, CH4, N2O, and other greenhouse gas emissions by sector (energy, industry, land use); (b) **Carbon price** -- the shadow price of carbon (in $/tCO2), a key policy variable that rises under ambitious climate scenarios; (c) **Energy** -- primary and final energy consumption by fuel type (coal, oil, gas, nuclear, renewables, biomass), energy prices; (d) **GDP** -- macroeconomic output (typically in PPP or market exchange rate terms); (e) **Investment** -- capital expenditure on energy supply infrastructure; (f) **Temperature** -- global mean temperature change relative to pre-industrial levels; (g) **Land use** -- area devoted to different uses (crops, forests, pasture).
- **Source**: https://data.ene.iiasa.ac.at/ngfs/#/downloads
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q1, Q3

## Fact 8
- **Claim**: In Phase IV (November 2023), the NGFS expanded its macro-financial variable suite through the NiGEM macroeconomic model (developed by NIESR). NiGEM translates IAM outputs into financial-system-relevant variables including: GDP growth, inflation (CPI), unemployment rate, short-term and long-term interest rates, exchange rates, equity prices, and government debt. These macro-financial variables are produced at the country level for major economies and are critical for bridging the gap between climate/energy scenarios and financial risk assessment.
- **Source**: https://www.ngfs.net/sites/default/files/medias/documents/ngfs_climate_scenarios_for_central_banks_and_supervisors_phase_iv.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q1, Q3

## Fact 9

Regional disaggregation varies by model and variable but generally covers major world regions (North America, Europe, China, etc.) and in some cases individual countries, particularly for macro-financial variables produced by NiGEM.
- **Source**: https://data.ene.iiasa.ac.at/ngfs/#/downloads

## Fact 10
- **Claim**: Carbon price is one of the most important distinguishing variables across NGFS scenarios. Under Net Zero 2050, carbon prices may rise to $200-$800+/tCO2 by 2050 depending on the model family. Under Current Policies, carbon prices remain near zero or at very low levels. Under Delayed Transition, carbon prices jump sharply around 2030 when policy is suddenly implemented, creating a price shock. This variable serves as a primary transmission channel for transition risk.
- **Source**: https://www.ngfs.net/sites/default/files/medias/documents/ngfs_climate_scenarios_for_central_banks_and_supervisors_phase_iv.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q1, Q3

## Fact 11
- **Claim**: GDP impacts vary significantly across NGFS scenarios. Orderly scenarios (Net Zero 2050, Below 2C) show relatively modest GDP losses relative to a no-climate-change baseline (on the order of a few percent by 2050). Disorderly scenarios (Delayed Transition, Divergent Net Zero) show larger short-to-medium-term GDP disruptions due to abrupt policy transitions. Hot house world scenarios (Current Policies) may show GDP losses from physical damages of 10-25% by end of century in the most severe damage function specifications, though these losses materialize primarily in the later decades (2060-2100).
- **Source**: https://www.ngfs.net/sites/default/files/medias/documents/ngfs_climate_scenarios_for_central_banks_and_supervisors_phase_iv.pdf
- **Confidence**: medium (exact magnitudes vary by model, damage function, and vintage)
- **Relevant to**: Q1, Q3

## Fact 12
- **Claim**: Climate risks transmit to bank loan portfolios through two primary channels: **physical risk** and **transition risk**. Physical risk affects lending through direct asset damage (property destruction from extreme weather), reduced collateral values, business interruption, supply chain disruption, and increased insurance costs. Transition risk affects lending through policy-driven cost increases (carbon taxes), stranded assets in fossil-fuel-related sectors, shifts in consumer demand, regulatory compliance costs, and macroeconomic disruption from structural economic transformation.
- **Source**: https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf (Acharya et al., "Climate Stress Testing," 2023)
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q2

## Fact 13
- **Claim**: Acharya et al. (2023) in "Climate Stress Testing" propose a framework where climate scenarios first affect the macroeconomy (GDP, unemployment, inflation, interest rates), and then macroeconomic conditions drive bank-level outcomes including loan losses, provisions, and capital adequacy. The approach parallels traditional macroeconomic stress testing (as in the Fed's CCAR/DFAST framework) but extends the scenario horizon and incorporates climate-specific drivers. The key innovation is connecting long-horizon climate pathways (30+ years) to the shorter-horizon financial risk metrics that banks and regulators use.
- **Source**: https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q2, Q3

## Fact 14
- **Claim**: The transmission mechanism from climate risk to bank credit operates through both **direct** and **indirect** channels. The direct channel: climate events or policies directly affect a borrower's ability to repay (e.g., a factory damaged by flooding cannot generate revenue; a coal company faces higher carbon costs). The indirect channel: climate risks affect the broader macroeconomy (lower GDP growth, higher unemployment, higher inflation), which in turn increases default rates across the entire loan portfolio, even for borrowers not directly exposed to climate risk.
- **Source**: https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q2

## Fact 15
- **Claim**: Jung et al. (2024) in "U.S. Banks' Exposures to Climate Transition Risks" find that U.S. banks have significant but heterogeneous exposures to climate transition risk through their commercial loan portfolios. Banks with higher concentrations of lending to carbon-intensive industries (oil and gas, mining, utilities, transportation, manufacturing) face larger potential losses under ambitious decarbonization scenarios. The paper estimates that transition risk could generate meaningful increases in expected credit losses for exposed portfolios.
- **Source**: https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1058.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q2

## Fact 16
- **Claim**: For **commercial and industrial (C&I) loans**, the key transmission channels are: (a) carbon pricing increases operating costs for carbon-intensive borrowers, reducing profitability and increasing default probability; (b) energy price shifts change input costs and competitive dynamics across sectors; (c) GDP growth slowdowns reduce business revenue and investment; (d) interest rate changes affect debt servicing costs; (e) physical damage to business assets and supply chains disrupts operations; (f) demand shifts away from carbon-intensive products reduce revenue for affected sectors.
- **Source**: https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1058.pdf; https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q2, Q3

## Fact 17
- **Claim**: For **consumer loans**, the key transmission channels are: (a) unemployment increases from economic restructuring reduce household income and ability to service debt; (b) inflation from carbon pricing erodes real purchasing power; (c) property value changes from physical risk affect collateral values for secured consumer credit; (d) energy cost increases reduce disposable income; (e) interest rate changes (both from monetary policy responses to climate-driven inflation and from climate risk premia) affect affordability of variable-rate consumer debt.
- **Source**: Synthesis from Acharya et al. (2023) and broader climate-finance literature (ECB climate stress test methodology)
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q2, Q3

## Fact 18
- **Claim**: The macroeconomic variables most commonly identified as mediating the climate-to-credit transmission are: (1) **GDP growth** -- the broadest measure of economic activity, directly linked to business revenue and household income; (2) **Unemployment rate** -- key driver of consumer default rates; (3) **Inflation / CPI** -- carbon pricing and energy transitions create cost-push inflation; (4) **Interest rates** (short-term and long-term) -- monetary policy responses to climate-driven inflation, plus risk premia; (5) **Carbon price** -- the direct policy instrument for transition risk; (6) **Energy prices** -- affect both business costs and household budgets.
- **Source**: https://www.ngfs.net/sites/default/files/medias/documents/ngfs_climate_scenarios_for_central_banks_and_supervisors_phase_iv.pdf; https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q3

## Fact 19
- **Claim**: The NGFS scenario framework uses a "cascade" or "waterfall" modeling approach: (1) IAMs (GCAM, REMIND, MESSAGEix) produce climate and energy system outputs (emissions, carbon prices, energy mix, temperature); (2) these outputs are fed into the NiGEM macroeconomic model to generate macro-financial variables (GDP, inflation, interest rates, unemployment); (3) banks and researchers then use these macro-financial variables as inputs to their own credit risk models to estimate loan losses. This three-stage cascade is the standard approach for climate stress testing.
- **Source**: https://www.ngfs.net/sites/default/files/medias/documents/ngfs_climate_scenarios_for_central_banks_and_supervisors_phase_iv.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q1, Q2, Q3

## Fact 20
- **Claim**: In the European Central Bank's 2022 climate stress test, banks were asked to project the impact of NGFS scenarios on their loan books. The exercise revealed that: (a) many banks lacked granular data on the climate vulnerability of their exposures; (b) short-term transition risks (disorderly scenarios) were generally found to create larger near-term credit losses than orderly scenarios; (c) physical risks, while potentially devastating in the long run, are harder to model because damage functions are highly uncertain and geographically concentrated.
- **Source**: https://www.bankingsupervision.europa.eu/ecb/pub/pdf/ssm.climate_stress_test_report.20220708~2e3cc0571f.en.pdf

## Fact 24
- **Claim**: A practical challenge for using NGFS data in loan portfolio forecasting is the frequency mismatch: NGFS scenarios provide annual data, while FRED loan data (BUSLOANS, CONSUMER) is available at monthly frequency. Researchers must either: (a) aggregate loan data to annual frequency to match NGFS, (b) interpolate NGFS annual data to monthly/quarterly frequency using methods such as cubic spline interpolation, linear interpolation, or Denton-Cholette temporal disaggregation, or (c) use a mixed-frequency modeling approach (e.g., MIDAS regression).
- **Source**: Project document and NGFS data portal documentation
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q1, Q3

## Fact 26
- **Claim**: The standard approach for climate stress testing of loan portfolios follows a two-step methodology: **Step 1** (historical relationship estimation): Use historical data to estimate the relationship between macroeconomic variables (GDP, unemployment, interest rates, inflation) and loan portfolio outcomes (loan growth, charge-off rates, delinquency rates). This typically uses time series methods such as VAR, VECM, or single-equation approaches (ADL, ARDL). **Step 2** (scenario-conditional forecasting): Plug the NGFS macro-financial scenario paths into the estimated model to generate conditional forecasts of loan portfolios under each climate scenario.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. (2023); ECB climate stress test methodology; Fed climate scenario analysis instructions
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q2, Q3

## Fact 27
- **Claim**: The relationship between GDP and bank lending is well-established empirically: loan growth is procyclical, meaning bank lending tends to expand during economic booms and contract during recessions. This makes GDP (and its climate-scenario-conditional paths) the single most important macroeconomic mediating variable for translating NGFS scenarios into loan portfolio forecasts. Studies consistently find GDP growth to be the most statistically significant predictor of aggregate loan volumes.
- **Source**: Federal Reserve stress testing documentation; Lown and Morgan (2006, "The Credit Cycle and the Business Cycle"); Bassett et al. (2014, "Changes in Bank Lending Standards")
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q3

## Fact 28
- **Claim**: Unemployment is a particularly important transmission variable for consumer loan portfolios. Higher unemployment directly reduces household income and increases the probability of default on consumer debt (auto loans, credit cards, personal loans). The relationship between unemployment and consumer loan delinquency is one of the most robust empirical findings in consumer credit research. Under NGFS scenarios with abrupt transitions, unemployment may spike temporarily as workers in carbon-intensive industries are displaced.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. (2023); Federal Reserve DFAST/CCAR methodology
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q3

## Fact 29
- **Claim**: Interest rates serve a dual role in the climate-to-lending transmission: (a) monetary policy response -- central banks may raise interest rates in response to climate-driven inflation (cost-push from carbon pricing, supply disruptions), which tightens financial conditions and reduces loan demand; (b) risk premia -- lenders may charge higher interest rates to borrowers perceived as exposed to climate risk, increasing the cost of credit and reducing loan volumes. Both channels tend to reduce aggregate lending under stress scenarios.
- **Source**: NGFS Phase IV documentation; Acharya et al. (2023)
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q3

## Fact 31
- **Claim**: Physical risk in the NGFS framework is quantified through climate damage functions that translate temperature increases into GDP losses. There is significant uncertainty in these damage functions: conservative estimates (such as Burke et al. 2015) suggest GDP losses of roughly 10-23% by 2100 under 3C+ warming, while more recent estimates incorporating non-linear tipping points suggest potentially larger damages. The NGFS uses multiple damage function specifications to capture this uncertainty.

## Summary of Key Findings

### For Q1 (NGFS Scenarios and Variables):
- Six scenarios in three categories: Orderly (Net Zero 2050, Below 2C), Disorderly (Divergent Net Zero, Delayed Transition), Hot House World (NDCs, Current Policies)
- Three IAM model families: GCAM, REMIND-MAgPIE, MESSAGEix-GLOBIOM
- Key variables: Carbon price, GDP, emissions, energy mix, temperature, plus NiGEM macro-financial outputs (inflation, unemployment, interest rates)
- Data is annual frequency, available through IIASA portal as CSV in ZIP format

### For Q2 (Transmission Mechanism):
- Two risk channels: physical risk (asset damage, business disruption) and transition risk (carbon pricing, stranded assets, policy costs)
- Direct channel: climate events/policies directly affect borrower repayment capacity
- Indirect channel: climate affects macroeconomy, which affects all borrowers
- Standard approach: NGFS scenarios -> macro variables -> credit risk models -> loan portfolio outcomes

### For Q3 (Mediating Macro Variables):
- **GDP growth**: most important single mediator; procyclical loan relationship
- **Unemployment rate**: critical for consumer loans; drives household default
- **Interest rates**: both monetary policy response and risk premia channels
- **Inflation/CPI**: carbon pricing creates cost-push inflation affecting real incomes
- **Carbon price**: direct policy instrument, primary transition risk variable
- **Energy prices**: affect business costs and household budgets

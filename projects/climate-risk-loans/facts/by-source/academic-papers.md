# Paper 1: Acharya et al. (2023) -- "Climate Stress Testing" (SR 1059)

## Fact 1
- **Claim**: Acharya et al. (2023) develop a comprehensive climate stress testing framework that translates climate scenarios (both physical and transition risk) into financial risk measures for banks, including impacts on asset values, credit losses, and bank capital.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. "Climate Stress Testing" (2023), https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q2, Q5, Q6

## Fact 2
- **Claim**: The paper identifies two distinct transmission channels for climate risk to the financial system: (1) physical risk, which affects asset values through property damage, reduced productivity, and disrupted supply chains; and (2) transition risk, which affects asset values through policy changes (carbon taxes, emissions regulations), technological shifts, and changes in consumer preferences.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. "Climate Stress Testing" (2023), https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q2

## Fact 3
- **Claim**: The climate stress testing framework uses NGFS climate scenarios as inputs, mapping them through integrated assessment models (IAMs) to macroeconomic variables, and then from macroeconomic variables to bank-level financial outcomes (credit losses, market losses, and capital depletion).
- **Source**: Acharya et al. "Climate Stress Testing" (2023), https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q2, Q5, Q6

## Fact 4
- **Claim**: The transmission chain in the framework is: Climate scenarios -> Integrated Assessment Models -> Macroeconomic variables (GDP, unemployment, interest rates, energy prices, carbon prices) -> Sector-level impacts -> Firm-level credit risk -> Bank portfolio losses -> Capital adequacy assessment. This multi-step "cascade" structure is the core methodological contribution.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. "Climate Stress Testing" (2023), https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q2, Q3, Q5

## Fact 5
- **Claim**: The key macroeconomic variables that mediate the climate-to-financial-risk transmission include: GDP growth, unemployment rate, inflation, short-term and long-term interest rates, energy prices (oil, gas, coal), carbon prices, and equity market valuations. These variables serve as the bridge between climate scenarios and bank credit risk models.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. "Climate Stress Testing" (2023), https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q3

## Fact 6
- **Claim**: The paper uses NGFS Phase III scenarios, including: Net Zero 2050 (orderly transition, early and gradual policy action, limiting warming to 1.5C), Delayed Transition (disorderly, late policy action starting around 2030 with abrupt carbon price increases), Below 2C (orderly but less ambitious than Net Zero), Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs, moderate policy), and Current Policies (no additional climate policy, highest physical risk).
- **Source**: Acharya et al. "Climate Stress Testing" (2023), https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q1, Q7

## Fact 7
- **Claim**: Under the Delayed Transition scenario, the abrupt imposition of carbon pricing around 2030 causes a sharp GDP decline and spike in unemployment, creating the most severe short-term financial stress for banks. This is because firms and banks have not had time to gradually adjust their portfolios and business models.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. "Climate Stress Testing" (2023), https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q7, Q8

## Fact 8
- **Claim**: The Net Zero 2050 scenario involves early, gradual policy tightening (smoothly rising carbon prices reaching several hundred dollars per ton by 2050), which produces moderate but manageable transition costs for the financial system because firms and banks can adjust over time.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. "Climate Stress Testing" (2023), https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q7

## Fact 9
- **Claim**: The Current Policies scenario (no additional climate action) results in the highest long-run physical risk costs, with GDP losses accumulating significantly by end-of-century due to climate damages, even though short-term transition costs are avoided.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. "Climate Stress Testing" (2023), https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf

## Fact 10
- **Claim**: The framework estimates bank credit losses by mapping macroeconomic scenario paths to changes in probabilities of default (PDs) and losses given default (LGDs) for different loan categories and sectors. Carbon-intensive sectors (energy, utilities, transportation, heavy manufacturing) show significantly higher PD increases under transition scenarios.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. "Climate Stress Testing" (2023), https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf

## Fact 11
- **Claim**: The paper finds that climate-related credit losses for the U.S. banking system could range from modest (under orderly Net Zero) to substantial (under disorderly Delayed Transition), with losses concentrated in fossil-fuel-dependent and carbon-intensive sectors. The Delayed Transition scenario produces the highest peak credit losses due to the abrupt policy shock.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. "Climate Stress Testing" (2023), https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf

## Fact 12
- **Claim**: The paper incorporates fire-sale externalities and amplification mechanisms: when banks suffer climate-related losses and must deleverage, forced asset sales can depress prices further, imposing additional losses on other banks holding similar assets. This creates a systemic risk channel beyond direct climate exposure.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. "Climate Stress Testing" (2023), https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q8

## Fact 13
- **Claim**: The fire-sale amplification mechanism means that total system-wide losses from climate shocks can exceed the sum of individual banks' direct climate losses, because mark-to-market accounting forces banks to recognize price declines caused by other banks' forced selling. This amplification is more severe when bank portfolios overlap heavily in climate-sensitive exposures.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. "Climate Stress Testing" (2023), https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q8

## Fact 14
- **Claim**: The methodology uses the NGFS scenario outputs from multiple integrated assessment models (including GCAM and REMIND-MAgPIE) and finds that results are sensitive to the choice of IAM, since different models produce different GDP paths, carbon price trajectories, and energy mix assumptions for the same scenario narrative.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. "Climate Stress Testing" (2023), https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf

## Fact 15
- **Claim**: A key limitation identified by the authors is the deep uncertainty inherent in climate projections: the economic models used to translate climate scenarios into macro outcomes are themselves uncertain, and compounding multiple layers of models (climate -> IAM -> macro -> credit) amplifies overall uncertainty. The authors stress that results should be interpreted as illustrative of relative risk magnitudes across scenarios rather than precise point forecasts.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. "Climate Stress Testing" (2023), https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q5, Q8

## Fact 16
- **Claim**: The paper notes that existing climate stress testing approaches (including those by the ECB, Bank of England, and the Fed's 2023 pilot) face common challenges: long time horizons (30+ years) that are unusual for traditional stress tests; the need to model structural economic transformation rather than cyclical downturns; and the lack of historical precedent for the types of shocks being modeled.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. "Climate Stress Testing" (2023), https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q6

## Fact 17
- **Claim**: The paper argues that traditional bank stress tests (which typically cover 1-3 year horizons and use historical macroeconomic relationships) are insufficient for climate risk because: (a) climate impacts unfold over decades, not quarters; (b) the structural nature of the transition means historical relationships between macro variables and credit losses may not hold; and (c) nonlinearities and tipping points in both climate and economic systems are difficult to capture with linear models.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. "Climate Stress Testing" (2023), https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q5, Q6

## Fact 18
- **Claim**: For transition risk specifically, the key transmission mechanism is: carbon pricing / regulation -> increased operating costs for carbon-intensive firms -> reduced profitability and cash flows -> higher probability of default -> increased credit losses on bank loans to those firms. Secondary effects include stranded assets (fossil fuel reserves that become uneconomic) reducing collateral values.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. "Climate Stress Testing" (2023), https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q2, Q3

## Fact 19
- **Claim**: For physical risk, the transmission mechanism is: increased frequency/severity of extreme weather -> property damage and business disruption -> reduced collateral values (especially for real estate-backed loans) and increased default rates -> credit losses. Physical risk is particularly relevant for commercial real estate (CRE) and residential mortgage portfolios in climate-vulnerable geographies.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. "Climate Stress Testing" (2023), https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q2, Q3

## Fact 20
- **Claim**: The authors propose using scenario-conditional forecasting as the core methodological approach: estimate the historical relationship between macroeconomic variables and bank outcomes (credit losses, loan growth), then use NGFS-scenario-implied macroeconomic paths as the conditioning variables for out-of-sample forecasting. This is analogous to the Federal Reserve's CCAR/DFAST methodology but adapted for climate horizons.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. "Climate Stress Testing" (2023), https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q5

## Fact 21

The frequency mismatch between annual NGFS data and quarterly/monthly banking data is identified as a practical challenge requiring interpolation or temporal aggregation.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. "Climate Stress Testing" (2023), https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1059.pdf

Most climate stress tests, including theirs, use a static or semi-static balance sheet assumption.

# Cross-Paper Findings (Both Papers Together)

## Fact 45
- **Claim**: Both papers collectively establish that the NGFS Delayed Transition scenario poses the greatest short-to-medium-term financial risk to U.S. banks because it combines the shock of an abrupt carbon price increase with insufficient lead time for firms and banks to adjust. The Net Zero 2050 (orderly) scenario produces lower peak losses but requires sustained adjustment over decades. The Current Policies scenario avoids transition costs but accumulates physical risk costs in the long run.
- **Source**: Both Acharya et al. (2023) and Jung et al. (2024)
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q7, Q8

## Fact 46
- **Claim**: Both papers identify GDP growth, unemployment, and carbon prices as the three most critical macroeconomic variables mediating the climate-to-bank-lending transmission. GDP captures the aggregate demand channel, unemployment captures the household income/consumer credit channel, and carbon prices capture the direct cost channel for transition risk.
- **Source**: Both Acharya et al. (2023) and Jung et al. (2024)
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q3

## Fact 47
- **Claim**: Both papers advocate for scenario-conditional forecasting as the appropriate methodological framework: estimate historical macro-to-credit relationships, then feed NGFS-scenario-implied macro paths through those estimated relationships to generate conditional forecasts of bank loan outcomes. This is conceptually similar to the Fed's existing CCAR stress testing approach but extended to climate-specific scenarios and longer horizons.
- **Source**: Both Acharya et al. (2023) and Jung et al. (2024)
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q5, Q6

## Fact 48
- **Claim**: Both papers note that the Fed conducted a pilot Climate Scenario Analysis (CSA) exercise in 2023 with the six largest U.S. bank holding companies, representing one of the first formal U.S. regulatory climate stress tests. This exercise used physical risk scenarios (hurricane impact on real estate) and transition risk scenarios (carbon pricing impact on corporate lending) but was described as exploratory rather than prescriptive.
- **Source**: Both Acharya et al. (2023) and Jung et al. (2024), referencing Fed Board of Governors CSA pilot
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q6

## Fact 49
- **Claim**: Both papers highlight that the European Central Bank (ECB) conducted its own climate stress test in 2022, finding that European banks' losses under adverse climate scenarios were manageable in aggregate but concentrated among banks with heavy exposures to carbon-intensive industries. The ECB exercise covered a 30-year horizon through 2050 under three NGFS scenarios.
- **Source**: Both papers reference ECB Climate Stress Test 2022 results
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q6

## Fact 50
- **Claim**: A common finding across both papers is that the lack of standardized, granular, and historically validated data on firms' carbon exposures is the single largest practical obstacle to climate stress testing. Emissions data is incomplete, often self-reported, and backward-looking, making it difficult to construct forward-looking credit risk models with confidence.
- **Source**: Both Acharya et al. (2023) and Jung et al. (2024)
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q4, Q5

## Fact 51
- **Claim**: Both papers use the NGFS scenario framework's categorization of scenarios along two dimensions: (1) level of climate ambition (how much warming is limited) and (2) policy transition pathway (orderly vs. disorderly). This 2x2-style framework helps distinguish whether the primary risk is from physical damages (low ambition) or transition shocks (high ambition, disorderly).
- **Source**: Both Acharya et al. (2023) and Jung et al. (2024)
- **Confidence**: high
- **Relevant to**: Q1, Q7

## Fact 52
- **Claim**: The implied policy recommendation from both papers is that early, gradual, and predictable climate policy action (orderly transition) is less destabilizing for the banking system than delayed, abrupt action (disorderly transition), even though both achieve similar long-term emissions reductions. This finding has important implications for financial stability: policy uncertainty itself is a source of financial risk.

## Source Notes
- Both papers are Federal Reserve Bank of New York Staff Reports and represent research views of the authors, not official Fed policy positions.
- Acharya et al. (2023) SR 1059 was initially circulated in 2023 and provides the broader theoretical and methodological framework for climate stress testing.
- Jung et al. (2024) SR 1058 was published in January 2024 and provides more empirical detail on U.S. banks' actual exposure distributions.
- Both papers use NGFS Phase III scenarios (released 2022-2023). The NGFS has since released updated scenarios (Phase IV), which may produce somewhat different quantitative results but use the same conceptual framework.
- The papers are complementary: Acharya et al. provides the framework and methodology, while Jung et al. provides the empirical measurement of exposures.
- Key limitation for this project: the papers focus on credit risk at the sector/firm level, whereas this project uses aggregate BUSLOANS and CONSUMER series from FRED. The aggregate data will not capture the sectoral composition effects that both papers emphasize. This means the project's forecasting model will necessarily capture only the macro-level transmission channels (GDP, unemployment affecting aggregate lending) rather than the sector-specific carbon exposure channel.
- Both papers' quantitative results depend heavily on the specific NGFS scenario vintage and IAM used; results could change with updated scenario releases.

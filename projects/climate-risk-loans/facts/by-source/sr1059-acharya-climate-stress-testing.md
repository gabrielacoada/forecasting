## Fact 1
- **Claim**: Climate stress tests, as employed for bank-level or regulatory (supervisory or macro-prudential) risk-management purposes, commonly have three components: scenario, model, and outcome (see Figure 1).
- **Source**: Acharya et al. (2023) "Climate Stress Testing", SR 1059, page 5
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q5, Q6

## Fact 2
- **Claim**: To design climate stress scenarios, both direct and indirect impacts of shocks are analyzed and both macro scenario variables (such as GDP and unemployment) and climate pathways (such as GHG emission and temperature evolution) are produced as the input to models in the next step.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. (2023) "Climate Stress Testing", SR 1059, page 5
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q1, Q3, Q5

## Fact 3
- **Claim**: Stress test outcomes include bank capital shortfalls, net interest income (NII), pre-provision net revenue (PPNR), and balance sheet (B/S) projections under the stress scenario. Outcomes can also include forward-looking market-based measures.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. (2023) "Climate Stress Testing", SR 1059, page 5
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q5, Q7

## Fact 4
- **Claim**: Climate-related risks to the economy are commonly categorized into two types: physical risks (risks to economic activity from direct manifestations of climate change, such as floods, heat waves, and wildfires) and transition risks (effects on economic activity from regulatory interventions, carbon emission taxes, renewable energy subsidies, or changes in technologies and preferences).
- **Source**: Acharya et al. (2023) "Climate Stress Testing", SR 1059, page 7
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q1, Q2

## Fact 5
- **Claim**: Krueger et al. (2020) find that among investment professionals, regulatory and technological risks are seen as somewhat more important than physical risks. Most respondents expect that regulatory climate risks are already important today, while physical risks are generally thought to only become important over longer horizons.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. (2023) "Climate Stress Testing", SR 1059, page 7
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q1, Q2

## Fact 6

## Fact 8

## Fact 9

These pathways are further fed into macro models to estimate the impact on macro indicators such as GDP, unemployment, and inflation.

## Fact 13

## Fact 14
- **Claim**: Net Zero 2050 is a scenario in which global warming is limited to 1.5C and global net zero CO2 emissions are achieved by about 2050. The Disorderly Transition scenarios involve higher transition risk due to delayed or divergent policy implementations. The Delayed Transition scenario assumes that annual emissions do not decrease until 2030.

## Fact 15
- **Claim**: NGFS (2021) outlines three sets of models that are typically employed to map scenario narratives to scenario variables: (1) climate impact models that take emission and economic exposure data and generate climate variables such as temperature and direct losses from natural disasters; (2) transition pathway models that take climate policy constraints and generate trajectories of carbon prices and temperature; and (3) economic impact models such as NiGEM and IAMs that take transition pathways and produce projections of key economic variables such as GDP, unemployment, and inflation.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. (2023) "Climate Stress Testing", SR 1059, page 10

## Fact 23
- **Claim**: The authors argue that transition risks arising from carbon taxes and renewable subsidies are dynamic choices made by policymakers who trade off the benefits and costs of various interventions while considering their political feasibility, and therefore scenarios involving extreme carbon taxes that significantly raise energy prices and lead to the bankruptcy of high-emissions industries may be deemed implausible in most countries.

## Fact 24
- **Claim**: A key challenge is that unlike for macroeconomic and financial risk factors with in-sample realizations that typify traditional bank stress tests, extreme physical or regulatory climate risks have not yet occurred, which minimizes the usefulness of statistical techniques to understand the relevant feedback loops. Understanding the relationship between climate risk realizations and GDP growth therefore requires the use of structural economic models.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. (2023) "Climate Stress Testing", SR 1059, page 13
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q4, Q5

## Fact 25
- **Claim**: In Dynamic Integrated Climate-Economy (DICE) models along the lines of Nordhaus and Boyer (2000), climate change is the byproduct of economic growth; damages from climate change are often modeled as a tax on consumption, and are thus largest when GDP levels are highest. In contrast, models along the lines of Weitzman (2012, 2014) and Barro (2015) consider low-probability catastrophic climate events where substantial physical risk and low GDP can occur jointly.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. (2023) "Climate Stress Testing", SR 1059, page 13
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q3, Q5

## Fact 26
- **Claim**: The concept of "compound risk" is important for climate stress testing: multiple risks may be realized at the same time. Compound risk scenarios may result when the realization of one risk subsequently increases the probability of another risk, creating causal ordering or feedback between the risks. The feedback loops between growth and climate risk are a potential source of compound risk.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. (2023) "Climate Stress Testing", SR 1059, page 14
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q1, Q2, Q5

## Fact 27
- **Claim**: The paper identifies three distinct channels through which climate risk affects banks (Figure 3): (1) Credit Risk through residential and corporate loans; (2) Market Risk through equities, bonds, and commodities; and (3) Liquidity Risk through inability to refinance. These are transmitted via lower property and corporate asset values, lower corporate profits, lower household wealth, and lower growth and productivity.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. (2023) "Climate Stress Testing", SR 1059, page 16
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF, adapted from Grippa et al. 2019 and Bolton et al. 2020)

## Fact 28
- **Claim**: Any quantitative assessment of the materiality of various climate risks for a bank's loan portfolio requires a comparison of the horizon over which these risks are expected to materialize and the average maturity of banks' loans. Recent work has documented that the average maturity of loans of U.S. banks at origination is between 3 and 5 years, with the average maturity in the case of European banks being closer to 5 years.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. (2023) "Climate Stress Testing", SR 1059, page 17
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)

## Fact 29
- **Claim**: Physical climate risk realizations can increase the credit risk exposure of banks through multiple channels: declining house prices due to sea level rise may induce mortgage defaults; cash flow shocks from floods and wildfires might reduce homeowners' ability to make mortgage payments; collateral values (land, machinery) can be negatively affected by abnormal temperatures; heat waves can raise energy expenditures with negative effects on consumer financial health; and supply chain disruptions can affect firms' ability to service bank loans.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. (2023) "Climate Stress Testing", SR 1059, page 17
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q2, Q3

## Fact 30
- **Claim**: To the extent that many of the cash-flow implications of physical climate risk realizations are expected to materialize only at longer horizons, the effects of those risks on banks through their loan books may be limited. Painter (2020) documents that sea-level rise exposure is only priced in municipal bonds with maturities in excess of twenty years.

## Fact 52
- **Claim**: Jung et al. (2021) develop a market-based climate stress testing methodology built on a new measure called CRISK (Climate Risk), which can be constructed at high frequency using only publicly available data on the balance sheets of financial institutions. The methodology involves three steps: (1) propose a climate risk factor measurable at high frequency, (2) measure banks' stock return sensitivity to the climate risk factor ("climate beta"), and (3) compute expected capital shortfall conditional on climate stress.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. (2023) "Climate Stress Testing", SR 1059, page 28
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q5

## Fact 53
- **Claim**: The "stranded asset portfolio" used as a climate risk factor by Jung et al. (2021) goes long 30% in Energy Select Sector SPDR ETF (XLE) and 70% in VanEck Vectors Coal ETF (KOL) and shorts the SPDR S&P 500 ETF (SPY). This is motivated by the consideration that along most transition paths, many assets of coal and hydrocarbon energy firms would become "stranded."
- **Source**: Acharya et al. (2023) "Climate Stress Testing", SR 1059, page 28
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q5

## Fact 54
- **Claim**: Formally, the CRISK of bank i at time t is: CRISK_it = kD_it - (1-k)W_it(1 - LRMES_it), where k denotes the prudential level of capital relative to assets, D denotes the bank's book value of debt, and W denotes the market capitalization of the bank. The prudential level of market equity capital relative to quasi-market value of assets can be set as a stress testing parameter, say 8%.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. (2023) "Climate Stress Testing", SR 1059, page 29
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q5

## Fact 55
- **Claim**: The climate betas in the CRISK framework are estimated dynamically, allowing for time-varying state-dependent volatility and correlation, based on the Dynamic Conditional Beta model in Engle (2002, 2009).
- **Source**: Acharya et al. (2023) "Climate Stress Testing", SR 1059, page 28
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q5

## Fact 56
- **Claim**: Jung et al. (2021) validate the CRISK approach using granular Y-14 data on large U.S. banks' loan portfolios. They estimate the climate beta of each 3-digit NAICS code industry and compute a weighted average climate beta for each bank. They show that loan-weighted climate betas are strongly aligned with top-down climate betas based only on market data of stock returns.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. (2023) "Climate Stress Testing", SR 1059, pages 29-30

## Fact 58
- **Claim**: The CRISK framework has a key limitation: it can measure the expected capital shortfall only to the extent that the market is pricing the climate risk and does not reflect underpriced or overpriced risks. The authors note that it is hard to imagine why financial markets should have better data on banks' climate risk exposures than banks themselves.
- **Source**: Acharya et al. (2023) "Climate Stress Testing", SR 1059, page 29

## Fact 59
- **Claim**: The stress testing cascade flow (Figure 1) shows: Scenario narratives (exogenous shocks, transmission channels, time horizon) feed into scenario models that have both direct impacts (firm-level exposure, frequency/intensity of shocks) and indirect impacts via a climate model (GHG emission, temperature, other) feeding into a macro model to generate macro indicators (GDP, unemployment, other). These then feed into risk modelling (interest rate risk, credit risk, market risk, other risks) and loss projection (loan losses, trading portfolio loss, insurance claim payouts).
- **Source**: Acharya et al. (2023) "Climate Stress Testing", SR 1059, page 6 (Figure 1)
- **Confidence**: high (directly from PDF)
- **Relevant to**: Q2, Q3, Q5

## Fact 60

They suggest complementing the balance-sheet or bottom-up approach of regulatory stress tests with a market-value or top-down approach.
